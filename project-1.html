Courier Item Substitutions

Role

Product Designer

Timeline

Jan – Mar 2023

Platform

Mobile (iOS / Android)

Skills

UX Design

Prototyping

User Research

Overview

Reducing friction for couriers when items are unavailable.

When a customer orders an item that turns out to be out of stock, the courier is put in a difficult position at the point of delivery. This project focused on redesigning the substitution flow to give couriers a clear, fast path forward — minimising cognitive load during a time-pressured task.

Speed

Couriers need to resolve substitutions in seconds, not minutes.

Clarity

The interface had to be scannable under stress and with gloves on.

Trust

Couriers need confidence that their choice is protected and logged.

Problem

Couriers were abandoning deliveries due to substitution confusion.

The existing flow presented substitution choices through a generic list with no visual hierarchy. Couriers working in noisy, time-pressured environments reported it as one of the most stressful parts of the job. Drop-off abandonment rates were measurably higher on orders containing substitutable items.

Couriers also had no way to communicate their choice to the customer in the moment — the only recourse was to call, adding further delay.

Research

Ride-alongs revealed where the flow broke down.

I joined couriers on delivery shifts to observe the substitution flow in real conditions. The key insight: couriers weren't reading item descriptions — they were pattern-matching on images and price. The existing UI forced them to read when they needed to scan.

8 couriers

Shadowed across urban and suburban routes

3 failure modes

Identified in the existing substitution flow

12% drop rate

On deliveries requiring substitution

Solution

A scan-first interface that leads with visuals and defaults to safe choices.

The redesigned flow surfaced the suggested substitution prominently with a large product image, a clear price difference indicator, and a single-tap accept. Declining required a deliberate second step — reducing accidental refusals.

The interface adapted to both portrait and landscape orientations for different courier device setups, and worked fully offline for areas with poor signal.

Outcome

Drop-off abandonment on substitution orders fell by 9%.

After shipping to 100% of couriers, substitution-related support tickets dropped significantly. Courier satisfaction scores on the substitution task increased in the following quarter. The pattern established here was later adopted as the standard for all courier exception flows.
