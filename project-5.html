Overview

A component library and token system built to scale across multiple product surfaces.

As the product organisation grew, inconsistency compounded. Different teams had built similar components in different ways. Designers and engineers were making local decisions that should have been global. This project established a shared design system — tokens, components, documentation, and the processes to keep it alive.

Consistency

One source of truth for visual decisions across all surfaces.

Speed

Reduce the time from idea to shipped interface.

Maintainability

A system that can evolve without breaking what already exists.

Principles

A system should serve the product, not constrain it.

The guiding principle was that the design system should make it faster to do the right thing, not slower to do anything. Components are opinionated enough to enforce consistency but flexible enough to accommodate real product variation. Documentation explains the why behind each decision, not just the what.

We also committed early to treating the system as a product itself — with a roadmap, versioning, and a dedicated support channel — rather than a side project maintained on goodwill.

Tokens

Semantic tokens create a shared vocabulary between design and code.

The token architecture was built in two layers: primitive tokens (raw values like specific hex colours and spacing increments) and semantic tokens (purpose-driven aliases like `color.action.primary` or `spacing.component.gap`). Semantic tokens are what designers and engineers reference in practice — they carry intent, not just value.

Colour

Full palette with semantic intent aliases for every use case.

Typography

Type scale, weights, and line heights as named styles.

Spacing

4pt grid system with named spacing tokens for layout and components.

Components

Built for the real conditions of the product, not the ideal ones.

Components were prioritised by frequency of use and cost of inconsistency. The first release covered 24 components — from buttons and inputs through to cards, modals, and navigation patterns. Each component was documented with usage guidance, anatomy, and explicitly labelled do/don't examples.

Every component shipped simultaneously in Figma and as a React component, keeping design and engineering in sync from day one rather than retrofitting later.

Rollout

Adoption required more than documentation — it required advocacy.

The system launched with dedicated office hours, a Slack channel, and a contribution model that let product teams propose additions. Adoption was tracked per-team and surfaced in a monthly system health dashboard. Within six months, 11 of 14 product teams had migrated their primary surfaces to system components.
