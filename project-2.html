Shop+Pay: The Checkout Experience

Overview

The checkout experience had to be the simplest thing in the courier's day.

By the time a courier reaches the checkout, the hardest part should be done. But checkout introduced its own set of challenges — barcodes to scan, bags to account for, a Visa card to tap, and potentially multiple orders to pay for in sequence. All of this happening at a register, with a cashier waiting and a line forming behind them. The checkout experience had to be the simplest thing in the courier's day.

Problem

Checkout is the highest-pressure moment in the entire Shop+Pay flow.

Unlike shopping — where a courier has relative freedom to move at their own pace — checkout is public, time-constrained, and unforgiving. Every unnecessary tap, every moment of confusion, every screen that wasn't immediately clear added friction at exactly the wrong time. We also needed to support pooled orders, meaning a single courier might complete checkout for multiple separate customer orders back to back, compounding that pressure further.

Challenge

Several distinct steps in a specific order — with no room for error.

Checkout required the courier to complete several distinct steps in a specific order: scan a Skip barcode to apply tax adjustments, add any paid bags to the order, pay using their Skip Visa card through their phone's wallet, and bag the items. Each step was non-negotiable — missing any one of them had downstream consequences for the order, the customer, or Skip's financials. The design had to sequence these steps clearly without feeling like a checklist, and keep the courier moving forward with absolute confidence at every moment.

Post-launch investigation surfaced a recurring problem: couriers were frequently attempting to purchase items that weren't part of the original order — most commonly rejected substitution items that had been left in the basket. Because the Skip Visa card is pre-loaded with the exact order total, any additional items caused the payment to be declined at the register. This created friction at the worst possible moment and contributed to a measurable increase in failed checkouts.

To address this, I introduced an order review screen at the start of the checkout flow. Before reaching the register, couriers are presented with a clear view of what should be in their basket and what needs to be removed. This gave couriers the confidence to catch discrepancies before they became a problem — and directly reduced declined payments as a result.

Pooled Orders

One courier, multiple customers, one register.

Skip supports pooled orders — a courier may arrive at checkout carrying items for more than one customer simultaneously. This introduced a layer of complexity that most checkout experiences never have to account for: how does a courier know which items belong to which order? How does payment work across multiple transactions in a row? The experience needed to handle this gracefully, keeping each order clearly separated without slowing the courier down or creating room for error.

Explorations

Keeping checkout fast — without cutting corners.

I worked closely with data analytics to understand where couriers were spending the most time, where errors were occurring, and which steps were generating support tickets. That data shaped which problems were worth solving and in what order.

Cross-functional input was equally critical — Business Operations, Customer Care, and Engineering each brought constraints the design had to account for. The goal throughout was simple: every step needed to be as fast as possible without creating room for error.

Speed and accuracy had to coexist in an environment where a courier can't afford to stop and read.
