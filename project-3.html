Overview

Designing a new category of delivery , from a blank canvas to a live product.

Skip is Canada's leading food delivery platform. Couriers receive an order, pick it up from a restaurant, and deliver it , simple. Shop+Pay changed that entirely. For the first time, couriers would walk into a store and shop for the customer themselves. No restaurant preparing the bag. No one handing them anything at the door.

I was the sole designer on this product — designing it from concept to launch and owning the maintenance of all features alongside a large development team.

Problem

An app built for pickup and drop-off , suddenly asked to support an entirely different job.

Skip's entire courier experience was built around a single interaction: pickup and drop off. Shop+Pay introduced a category of complexity that the app simply wasn't built for. Couriers needed to navigate an unfamiliar store, find specific items across dozens of aisles, manage quantities and weights, and make real-time decisions , all while on the clock.

The existing product gave them nothing to work with.

Getting Organized

The Process

Shop+Pay wasn't a feature request — it was a business directive that would fundamentally change how Skip operated. When the ask landed, there was no blueprint. No existing flow to build on. No comparable feature in our own product to reference.

Before any design work began, I ran workshops with cross-functional stakeholders across the business — Business Operations, Customer Care, and beyond. Each team brought a different lens: ops cared about order reliability, customer care surfaced the edge cases they knew would generate tickets, business leads were focused on partner requirements and launch viability. My job was to hold all of that together and make sure every piece of the puzzle was accounted for before a single screen was designed.

From there, I worked closely alongside developers throughout the entire process — not just at handoff. That relationship was critical on a project this size, where technical constraints shaped design decisions early and often.

Challenge

Designing for a courier mid-shop , moving, under time pressure, one hand on their phone.

A courier mid-shop is in a fundamentally different context than any existing flow. They're moving through a physical space, under time pressure, often on a phone with one hand. The shopping experience had to work with that reality , not against it.

Every screen needed to be scannable, every action fast, and every decision the courier had to make needed to be surfaced at exactly the right moment.

Speciality Items

Weighted Items

Not every item on a grocery list has a fixed price. Produce, deli meats, bulk goods — these are sold by weight, and supporting them was a non-trivial design problem. Couriers needed a way to shop for items the same way any customer would at a grocery store: selecting by weight at the counter or scale, and having that reflected accurately in the order.

Getting this right wasn't just a UX improvement — it was a business unlock. Supporting weighted items opened the door to major grocery partnerships that weren't possible before.

Walmart — one of Canada's largest retailers
Loblaws — a rollout across 1,000+ locations across Canada
Together, these partnerships represent an estimated $143M in GTV for Skip. Designing a shopping experience that couriers could trust with this level of nuance directly enabled that growth.

Pooled Orders

Every pain point in a single order is amplified across two or three.

Shop+Pay supports pooled orders — couriers can shop for more than one customer at a time. On paper, it's an efficiency gain. In practice, it multiplies every design challenge in the shopping experience. Any friction or confusion that exists in a single order becomes significantly harder to manage across two or three. The mental load of tracking multiple lists, across multiple customers, inside a single store visit is real.

The principle that guided this: the core Shop+Pay experience had to be nearly seamless before we could responsibly introduce pooled orders. Every pain point that existed in a single-order flow would be amplified. The shopping experience had to be simple and fluid enough to support the way couriers naturally think — one item at a time, one clear next step — before we layered in the complexity of multiple simultaneous orders.

Reflection

Shipping a 0→1 product as the sole designer inside a company that had never built anything like it before required more than design craft — it required a different kind of designer entirely.

Ownership sharpened my ability to prioritize and move with confidence in ambiguity. Every decision started and ended with me.

Collaboration was the backbone of the project. Aligning Business Ops, Customer Care, and a large dev team around a shared vision meant learning to speak every team's language and earn their trust — not just design great screens.

Growth came from betting on a big idea and seeing it through. Watching Shop+Pay go from a concept I introduced to Skip to a product with $44M in GTV and nearly a million completed orders is the reminder that the best design work happens when you take ownership of the whole problem.
