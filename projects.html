Overview

Designing a new category of delivery — from a blank canvas to a live product.

Skip is Canada's leading food delivery platform. Couriers receive an order, pick it up from a restaurant, and deliver it — simple. Shop+Pay changed that entirely. For the first time, couriers would walk into a store and shop for the customer themselves. No restaurant preparing the bag. No one handing them anything at the door.

I was the sole designer on this product — designing it from concept to launch and owning the maintenance of all features alongside a large development team.

Problem

An app built for pickup and drop-off — suddenly asked to support an entirely different job.

Skip's entire courier experience was built around a single interaction: pickup and drop off. Shop+Pay introduced a category of complexity that the app simply wasn't built for. Couriers needed to navigate an unfamiliar store, find specific items across dozens of aisles, manage quantities and weights, and make real-time decisions — all while on the clock.

The existing product gave them nothing to work with.

Research

Couriers weren't just tolerating this new offer type — many actively preferred it.

Before designing anything, I needed to understand who I was designing for and whether there was appetite for this type of work.

50%

of Skip couriers expressed interest in Shop+Pay offers

60%

of those who tried it complete at least one S+P order per week

We asked couriers what they value most about Shop+Pay offers — the results showed a workforce that's genuinely bought in, not just along for the ride.

"The grocery shopping is honestly fun, it's like having a little treasure hunt to do and it's not difficult work."

Getting Organized

The Process

Shop+Pay wasn't a feature request — it was a business directive that would fundamentally change how Skip operated. When the ask landed, there was no blueprint. No existing flow to build on. No comparable feature in our own product to reference.

Before any design work began, I ran workshops with cross-functional stakeholders across the business — Business Operations, Customer Care, and beyond. Each team brought a different lens: ops cared about order reliability, customer care surfaced the edge cases they knew would generate tickets, business leads were focused on partner requirements and launch viability. My job was to hold all of that together and make sure every piece of the puzzle was accounted for before a single screen was designed.

From there, I worked closely alongside developers throughout the entire process — not just at handoff. That relationship was critical on a project this size, where technical constraints shaped design decisions early and often.

Challenge

Designing for a courier mid-shop — moving, under time pressure, one hand on their phone.

A courier mid-shop is in a fundamentally different context than any existing flow. They're moving through a physical space, under time pressure, often on a phone with one hand. The shopping experience had to work with that reality — not against it.

Every screen needed to be scannable, every action fast, and every decision the courier had to make needed to be surfaced at exactly the right moment.

Speciality Items

Weighted Items

Not every item on a grocery list has a fixed price. Produce, deli meats, bulk goods — these are sold by weight, and supporting them was a non-trivial design problem. Couriers needed a way to shop for items the same way any customer would at a grocery store: selecting by weight at the counter or scale, and having that reflected accurately in the order.

Getting this right wasn't just a UX improvement — it was a business unlock. Supporting weighted items opened the door to major grocery partnerships that weren't possible before.

Walmart — one of Canada's largest retailers
Loblaws — a rollout across 1,000+ locations across Canada
Together, these partnerships represent an estimated $143M in GTV for Skip. Designing a shopping experience that couriers could trust with this level of nuance directly enabled that growth.

Pooled Orders

Every pain point in a single order is amplified across two or three.

Shop+Pay supports pooled orders — couriers can shop for more than one customer at a time. On paper, it's an efficiency gain. In practice, it multiplies every design challenge in the shopping experience. Any friction or confusion that exists in a single order becomes significantly harder to manage across two or three. The mental load of tracking multiple lists, across multiple customers, inside a single store visit is real.

The principle that guided this: the core Shop+Pay experience had to be nearly seamless before we could responsibly introduce pooled orders. Every pain point that existed in a single-order flow would be amplified. The shopping experience had to be simple and fluid enough to support the way couriers naturally think — one item at a time, one clear next step — before we layered in the complexity of multiple simultaneous orders.

Reflection

Shipping a 0→1 product as the sole designer inside a company that had never built anything like it before required more than design craft — it required a different kind of designer entirely.

Ownership sharpened my ability to prioritize and move with confidence in ambiguity. Every decision started and ended with me.

Collaboration was the backbone of the project. Aligning Business Ops, Customer Care, and a large dev team around a shared vision meant learning to speak every team's language and earn their trust — not just design great screens.

Growth came from betting on a big idea and seeing it through. Watching Shop+Pay go from a concept I introduced to Skip to a product with $44M in GTV and nearly a million completed orders is the reminder that the best design work happens when you take ownership of the whole problem.

User Research

Prototyping

Overview

Three options at checkout — giving customers the right amount of control, without adding friction.

Grocery shopping is personal. The brand you buy, the ripeness you prefer, the things you'd never swap. When we built Shop+Pay, out-of-stock items were inevitable. Substitutions gave customers a moment at checkout to decide how they want to handle it — with best match selected by default, so most customers don't have to do anything at all.

Problem

12% of items were going out of stock with no structured way for customers to handle it.

Before Substitutions launched, 12% of items in Shop+Pay orders were going out of stock with no structured way to resolve it. Couriers were left making judgment calls, customers were caught off guard, and the experience felt unpredictable. For a product built around trust — handing off your grocery list to a stranger — unpredictability was a problem we couldn't leave unsolved.

Research

Customers wanted control — and they were willing to take a moment upfront to get it.

Grocery shopping carries a level of personal preference that restaurant orders simply don't. To design the right solution, we needed to understand how much control customers actually wanted — and whether they'd use it.

We conducted moderated interviews to understand how customers thought about item substitutions — what felt acceptable, what felt intrusive, and where they wanted to be involved. Those findings were then validated at scale through surveys, ensuring the themes we heard in interviews weren't outliers. Finally, we brought the proposed options back to couriers to confirm the experience would work for both sides of the transaction — the choices customers make have a direct impact on how couriers do their job.

45% of customers preferred to set substitution preferences at checkout rather than deal with it mid-order.

Set it and forget it — customers said the ability to set preferences upfront made the overall experience feel less anxious.

Four themes emerged consistently across every conversation:

Control

Customers wanted to make the call themselves — not leave it to chance.

Transparency

They wanted to know what was happening with their order, not be surprised after the fact.

Choice

Different customers wanted different levels of involvement — the experience needed to support all of them.

Ease

Whatever the solution, it couldn't feel like extra work.

Getting Organized

The Process

Challenge

A decision at checkout about a problem that may never happen.

The substitution moment sits at an uncomfortable intersection — customers are being asked at checkout to think through what happens if an item goes out of stock during a shop that hasn't started yet. Ask for too much and it feels overwhelming. Ask for too little and customers feel out of control when something does go wrong. The experience had to feel light and optional while still giving customers who wanted it a meaningful way to set their preferences.

"I don't want to grocery shop twice"

Solution

Three options at checkout. Best match by default.

At checkout, customers see a substitution preference attached to each item in their cart. The selection is optional — best match is pre-selected so customers who don't want to engage don't have to.

Best match (default)

The courier sends the customer real-time substitution suggestions while shopping. The customer approves or rejects each one before the item goes in the cart.

Choose a replacement now

The customer picks a specific backup item at checkout. If their original is out of stock, the courier swaps it automatically — no interruption needed.

Refund

If the item is out of stock, the customer is refunded. No replacement, no message — a clean resolution.

The Experience

Customer and courier screens reflect each other — what a customer chooses shapes exactly what the courier sees.

Option 1: Best match

Customer experience

At checkout, best match is pre-selected on each cart item. Customers who want more control can tap to change it, but most will move on without a second thought. If an item goes out of stock during the shop, the customer receives a notification with options the courier has sent from the store. They approve or reject directly from the notification — no need to open the app.

Courier experience

When a courier marks an item as out of stock, they see that the customer has best match selected. Previously, couriers had to manually search the store for alternatives themselves — a time-consuming process that contributed to a ~50% substitution rejection rate. Couriers were guessing, and customers were feeling it.

To address this, we're introducing AI-powered substitution recommendations directly into the picking flow. When an item goes out of stock, couriers receive a curated list of five smart suggestions — filtered by budget, inventory availability, dietary restrictions, and proximity in the store — so the right alternatives surface instantly, without the manual search.

  • 5 AI-generated suggestions per out-of-stock item
  • Budget-aware and inventory-accurate filtering
  • Dietary compliance and restricted item safety — no alcohol or cannabis substitutions
  • Proximity-prioritized to keep picking fast

The result: less guesswork for couriers, better suggestions for customers, and a direct path to improving the substitution acceptance rate and overall fill rate. This also lays the foundational AI infrastructure for future recommendation features across the Shop+Pay experience.

During shop

As the courier shops, the customer receives a push notification for each out-of-stock item. The notification surfaces the courier's suggested substitution and gives the customer a single tap to approve or reject — no need to open the app. If approved, the item goes straight into the cart. If rejected, the courier moves on without it.

Option 2: Choose a replacement now

Customer experience

At checkout, the customer taps the item and selects a specific replacement from the catalog. Their choice is saved to the order before the shop begins.

For customers who want full control, this option delivers exactly that. At checkout, they tap the item and select a specific replacement from the catalog — their choice is locked in before the shop begins. No notifications mid-order, no decisions under pressure, no surprises at the door.

This speaks directly to what research told us customers valued most: control, transparency, choice, and ease. The ability to pre-select a replacement is the purest expression of all four — the customer has made their decision on their own terms, in their own time, and the order carries it forward without any further input needed. Set it and forget it, exactly as they asked for.

Courier experience

If the original item is out of stock, the courier immediately sees the customer's pre-selected replacement. No message sent, no wait — they pick the replacement and continue shopping without interruption.

Results

Out-of-stock items dropped from 12% to 4% — a 67% reduction in unresolved stockouts.

12% → 4%

Out-of-stock rate after launch

67%

Reduction in unresolved stockouts

Reflection

Pre-order decisions can dramatically improve in-order experience — but only when the upfront ask feels proportionate to the benefit.

Getting that balance right was the core design challenge here. The best outcome was a feature most customers would barely notice — because the default did the work for them.

User Research

Prototyping

Overview

The checkout experience had to be the simplest thing in the courier's day.

By the time a courier reaches the checkout, the hardest part should be done. But checkout introduced its own set of challenges — barcodes to scan, bags to account for, a Visa card to tap, and potentially multiple orders to pay for in sequence. All of this happening at a register, with a cashier waiting and a line forming behind them. The checkout experience had to be the simplest thing in the courier's day.

Problem

Checkout is the highest-pressure moment in the entire Shop+Pay flow.

Unlike shopping — where a courier has relative freedom to move at their own pace — checkout is public, time-constrained, and unforgiving. Every unnecessary tap, every moment of confusion, every screen that wasn't immediately clear added friction at exactly the wrong time. We also needed to support pooled orders, meaning a single courier might complete checkout for multiple separate customer orders back to back, compounding that pressure further.

Regular Checkout

Every step sequenced, every action confirmed, every order accounted for.

A walkthrough of the shipped flow — from the moment a courier approaches the register to the moment they pick up the bags and head to the customer.

Challenge

Several distinct steps in a specific order — with no room for error.

Checkout required the courier to complete several distinct steps in a specific order: scan a Skip barcode to apply tax adjustments, add any paid bags to the order, pay using their Skip Visa card through their phone's wallet, and bag the items. Each step was non-negotiable — missing any one of them had downstream consequences for the order, the customer, or Skip's financials. The design had to sequence these steps clearly without feeling like a checklist, and keep the courier moving forward with absolute confidence at every moment.

Post-launch investigation surfaced a recurring problem: couriers were frequently attempting to purchase items that weren't part of the original order — most commonly rejected substitution items that had been left in the basket. Because the Skip Visa card is pre-loaded with the exact order total, any additional items caused the payment to be declined at the register. This created friction at the worst possible moment and contributed to a measurable increase in failed checkouts.

To address this, I introduced an order review screen at the start of the checkout flow. Before reaching the register, couriers are presented with a clear view of what should be in their basket and what needs to be removed. This gave couriers the confidence to catch discrepancies before they became a problem — and directly reduced declined payments as a result.

Pooled Orders

One courier, multiple customers, one register.

Skip supports pooled orders — a courier may arrive at checkout carrying items for more than one customer simultaneously. This introduced a layer of complexity that most checkout experiences never have to account for: how does a courier know which items belong to which order? How does payment work across multiple transactions in a row? The experience needed to handle this gracefully, keeping each order clearly separated without slowing the courier down or creating room for error.

Explorations

Keeping checkout fast — without cutting corners.

I worked closely with data analytics to understand where couriers were spending the most time, where errors were occurring, and which steps were generating support tickets. That data shaped which problems were worth solving and in what order.

Cross-functional input was equally critical — Business Operations, Customer Care, and Engineering each brought constraints the design had to account for. The goal throughout was simple: every step needed to be as fast as possible without creating room for error.

Speed and accuracy had to coexist in an environment where a courier can't afford to stop and read.

Product Reflection

Nobody had done this before — not us, not the team, not Skip. We were building it as we went.

Shop+Pay wasn't an evolution of something that existed. There was no playbook to pull from, no comparable feature in our own product to reference, no one in the building who had shipped something like this before. That was true for me, for the product managers, for the developers, for the operations team. We were all learning together in real time.

What made it work was the team. Cross-functional collaboration wasn't a process we followed — it was a survival mechanism. Every edge case we hadn't anticipated, every operational constraint that surfaced mid-build, every moment where the design had to flex around a technical reality — none of it got resolved in isolation. We were constantly in each other's work, and that closeness made the product better.

The scale of what we shipped together still catches me off guard. Nearly a million completed orders added to the network. Couriers who had never done anything other than restaurant pickup now walking into grocery stores, shopping for strangers, and doing it well. A product that didn't exist — conceived, designed, built, and shipped by a team that was figuring it out alongside each other.

That's the version of product work I want to keep doing: the kind where the answer isn't already known, where the team has to earn the outcome together, and where the result is something genuinely new in the world.
