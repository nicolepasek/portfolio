Overview

Designing a first-run experience that activates new users without overwhelming them.

A user's first session is the highest-leverage moment in the product lifecycle. This project redesigned the new user onboarding flow — from sign-up through to first completed order — with the goal of increasing activation rate and reducing time-to-first-order.

Activate fast

Get users to their first order as quickly as possible.

Build confidence

New users need to feel the product is reliable before committing.

Ask less, personalise more

Collect preferences progressively rather than upfront.

Problem

New users were dropping off before placing their first order.

The previous onboarding required users to complete a lengthy setup flow — including address, dietary preferences, and payment — before they could browse restaurants. Many users abandoned before reaching the home screen. Those who made it through had a high first-session order rate, but the top-of-funnel loss was significant.

The core tension: the product needed certain data to work well, but asking for it all upfront was killing activation.

Research

Competitive analysis and drop-off data told two different stories.

Competitor benchmarking showed that leading delivery apps had moved to address-first onboarding — getting users to browse before asking anything else. Our own data confirmed the same pattern: users who reached the restaurant list within the first 60 seconds had a 3× higher first-order rate than those who took longer.

6 competitor apps

Benchmarked for onboarding patterns and friction points

60-second threshold

Users reaching the restaurant list within 60s converted at 3× the rate

42% drop-off

On the preferences step before the home screen

Solution

Address-first onboarding with progressive preference collection.

The new flow asked for only one thing upfront: a delivery address. This immediately unlocked the restaurant browse experience. Dietary preferences were collected inline during the first order — surfaced as a natural part of the ordering flow rather than a setup chore. Payment was deferred entirely to checkout.

The three-screen sequence — address, brief value proposition, restaurant list — was validated in prototype testing with 12 new users before being built.

Outcome

First-order activation increased by 18% in the month after launch.

The redesigned flow reduced median time-to-restaurant-list from 2 minutes 40 seconds to under 45 seconds. The drop-off on the preferences step was effectively eliminated. The progressive preference collection approach was later adopted across other setup flows in the app.
